The following workflow uses getCensus() to prepare the data sets for the analysis. The ACS data is analyzed on the level of census tracts and is referring to 2018. We are interested in the data for California; more specifically, the nine Bay Area counties. The analysis is done on the level of households.

The next step is to choose the outcome variables to be used for the regression. There is one dependent variable and two independent variables. All three are continuous and all three represent percentages.

The dependent variable is percentage of public transportation (excluding taxicab) as means of transportation to work, denoted as perc_pt. It was found by dividing public transportation values by the total values for means of transportation to work, as shown below using the ACS variables.

The first independent variable is percentage of white households. It was found in the same way as in the textbook and is denoted as perc_white here.

The second independent variable is percentage of househoolds with income less than $30k (in 2018 inflation-adjusted dollars). It was found using the five income buckets as shown below annd is denoted as perc_below30k.

The motivation for this choice of outcome and variables comes from reading about the Pew Research Center survey called “Who relies on public transit in the U.S.” It can be found at: https://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2016/04/07/who-relies-on-public-transit-in-the-u-s/. It found that “who is taking public transit varies substantially by region, nativity, and race and ethnicity.” Among the three racial/ethnic groups (White, Black, Hispanic), white people use public transportation the least. When it comes to income, those with the income of less than $30k were using public transportation the most.

bay_multiple_tract <- 
  getCensus(
    name = "acs/acs5",
    vintage = 2018,
    region = "tract:*",
    regionin = "state:06+county:001,013,041,055,075,081,085,095,097",
    vars = c(
      "B19001A_001E",
      "B19001_001E",
      
      "B08301_001E",
      "B08301_010E",
      
      "B19001_001E",
      "B19001_002E",
      "B19001_003E",
      "B19001_004E",
      "B19001_005E",
      "B19001_006E"
    )
  ) %>% 
  transmute(
    tract = paste0(state, county, tract),
    perc_white = B19001A_001E / B19001_001E,
    perc_pt = B08301_010E / B08301_001E,
    perc_below30k = (B19001_002E + B19001_003E + B19001_004E + B19001_005E + B19001_006E) / B19001_001E
  ) %>% 
  filter(
    !is.na(perc_white), 
    !is.na(perc_pt),
    !is.na(perc_below30k)
  )

Although not exactly centered at 0, the residuals are symmetric which is good.

Both independent variables have p-values much lower than 0.05 so both of them are statistically significant (we can reject the null hypothesis), as indicated by ***.

The coefficient of perc_white is negative which means that increase in perc_white is related to decrease in perc_pt, which agrees with the simple regression plot and the survey. The coefficient of perc_below30k is positive which means that increase in perc_below30k is related to increase in perc_pt which agrees with the simple regression analysis and the survey.

The R-squared value shows us that around 6% of variation in the dependent variable is explained by this model. This value is relatively low which indicates that these two independent variables are not enough to explain the use of public transportation. It is interesting to see that combining the two variables increased the R-squared value only a little bit, so the combination of race and income might not necessarily be much better in explaining public transportation use than income alone.

The regression analysis above indicates that race and income do not provide a full picture when it comes to understanding who uses public transportation the most and why. Therefore, no causal claims can be made. However, there might be possible explanations for some of the trends seen. The Pew Research Center survey suggests:

“One possible reason that blacks, Hispanics and immigrants might be bigger users of public transit is because they are more likely than Americans overall to live in large metropolitan areas, where there tend to be more public transit options. They are also less likely to have access to an automobile than other groups and are more likely to use public transit for commuting to work. Blacks and Hispanics also tend to live farther away from their jobs, which could make walking or biking to work less common.”

They are also careful to not claim any causal relationships while trying to suggest possible explanations.
